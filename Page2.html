How Greenland is Affected by Climate Change

A Summary

Positioned in the Arctic, Greenland's Ice Sheet has become the epicenter of climate change; and it's melting rapidly before our very eyes...
  • Greenland's ice sheet is currently melting 6-7 times faster than it was back in the 1980's.
  • Since 1993, Greenland has cumulatively lost 3.8 trillion tons of melted ice into the ocean.
  • One trillion ton being lost in between the years 2011-2014 alone.
  • Greenland is the biggest contributor to sea level rise.

These uncharacteristically warmer conditions surrounding Greenland and the Arctic means that it affects the local community comprised of indigenous hunters especially those that rely on sled dogs or snowmobiles to traverse across the vast fields of ice. This affects their livelihood and future generations of local indigenous hunters may be no more. It is likely that a continued trend of accelerated melting rates will cause the sea levels to rise even further and will inevitably pose many other serious ecological repercussions.

Human Causes: 'Global Warming and the Greenland Ice Sheet'

The great glacial melt is massively linked to human implications, we have become a great force of nature in melting the ice sheets. Scientific data show that since the industrial revolution, human activities have greatly intensified the melting of glaciers every time we burn fossil fuels. Humans have added a layer of excess carbon dioxide that lingers in our atmosphere trapping even more heat energy from the sun, forcing the earth's glaciers to retreat and disappear at unprecedented rates. Emissions of carbon dioxide Co2 are particulary connected to our need for heating, transportation, manufacturing and supplies. Deforestation is also a major source on the levels of carbon dioxide, less trees means that less carbon dioxide is absorbed from the earth's atmosphere.

Natural Causes

Positive Feedback Loops: The rise in recent global air and ocean temperatures due to the greenhouse effect have created and triggered a positive feedback loop that continually melts within the ice sheet in a vicious cycle. The ocean acts as a giant heat sink and absorbs more than 90% of the Earth's warmth. The earth's global temperature rises -> ice melts in greater proportions -> albedo drops which means less overall ice reflectivity -> more solar radiation is absorbed and soaked up by the open water -> further warming up waters -> melting the ice and the cycle repeats. -> The cyclical nature of this postive feedback loop has caused the melting rate has outpaced the recovery rate.

Algae Blooms: The darkening of the Greenland Ice Sheet is caused by algae blooms also known as 'glacier algae.' They normally live on the upper few centimetres towards the surface of the ice and have created many widespread blooms along the southwest regions of the ice sheet. Due to their protective darker pigmentation used as a survival mechanism it absorbs more sunlight energy that leads to increased melting rates when they collectively come together to create these colonies of 'Dark Zones' this becomes an amplified problem during the summer season. The proliferation of these microbial extremophiles are changing the reflectivity of the ice sheet, they are essentially capturing the sun's heat and light energy helping the ice to warm up and melt in rapid levels.

Melt Lakes and Streams: Because of the blue waters of the melt lakes and streams that form on the surface of the ice sheet, it absorbs the sunlight in greater amounts unlike the white ice that reflects the solar rays, contributing to a higher level of melting. Not only that, oftentimes the meltwater would seep and flow down through the cracks of the ice sheet all the way down to the bottom of the bedrock, melting the ice from the inside out, this may act as a lubricant for the glacier to slide which may cause an irreversible change.
